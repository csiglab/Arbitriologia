## Economic History

Here's a detailed table summarizing key periods and developments in Portugal's economic history:

| **Period** | **Key Events and Developments** |
| --- | --- |
| **Pre-15th Century** | - Predominantly agrarian economy with limited trade.<br>- Local crafts and small-scale industries. |
| **15th-16th Centuries** | - Age of Discoveries begins under Prince Henry the Navigator.<br>- Establishment of global trade routes.<br>- Wealth from colonies in Africa, Asia, and the Americas, leading to a prosperous economy. |
| **17th Century** | - Decline in global dominance due to competition from other European powers.<br>- Economic struggles despite gold from Brazil. |
| **18th Century** | - Marquis of Pombal's economic reforms post-1755 Lisbon earthquake.<br>- Modernization of industry and trade policies.<br>- Focus on wine production, particularly Port wine. |
| **19th Century** | - Loss of Brazilian colony in 1822.<br>- Slow industrialization compared to other European nations.<br>- Predominantly agrarian economy with some textile and cork industries. |
| **Early 20th Century** | - Political instability and economic difficulties.<br>- Estado Novo regime under Salazar from 1933, focusing on economic autarky.<br>- Limited industrial growth, reliance on agriculture and colonial resources. |
| **1940s-1970s** | - Post-WWII economic growth and modest industrialization.<br>- Continued dependence on colonies for raw materials.<br>- Economic challenges leading up to the Carnation Revolution in 1974. |
| **1974-1986** | - Transition to democracy post-Carnation Revolution.<br>- Decolonization leading to loss of overseas territories.<br>- Economic instability and high inflation. |
| **1986-2000** | - Joining the European Economic Community (EEC) in 1986.<br>- Structural reforms and modernization of the economy.<br>- Growth in tourism, services, and foreign investment. |
| **2000s-Present** | - Adoption of the Euro in 1999.<br>- Economic challenges during the 2008 global financial crisis.<br>- EU bailout in 2011 with subsequent austerity measures.<br>- Gradual economic recovery with emphasis on technology, renewable energy, and tourism. |

## Industrial Policy

Here's a detailed table summarizing key periods and developments in Portugal's industrial policy from 1500 onwards:

| Period | Key Policies and Developments |
| --- | --- |
| **1500-1600s** | - Age of Discoveries policies promoting maritime exploration and colonial expansion.<br>- Establishment of trade monopolies and exploitation of colonies for resources.<br>- Focus on shipbuilding and naval industry. |
| **1700s** | - Marquis of Pombal's economic reforms post-1755 Lisbon earthquake.<br>- Modernization of industry and trade policies.<br>- Establishment of manufacturing industries such as textiles and ceramics.<br>- Promoted wine production, particularly Port wine. |
| **1800s** | - Slow industrialization compared to other European nations.<br>- Predominantly agrarian economy with some textile and cork industries.<br>- Establishment of limited railways and infrastructure. |
| **Early 20th Century** | - Political instability and economic difficulties.<br>- Estado Novo regime under Salazar from 1933, focusing on economic autarky.<br>- Limited industrial growth, reliance on agriculture and colonial resources.<br>- Establishment of state-owned enterprises and industrial protectionism. |
| **1940s-1970s** | - Post-WWII economic growth and modest industrialization.<br>- Continued dependence on colonies for raw materials.<br>- Development of key industries such as textiles, footwear, and food processing.<br>- Infrastructure development, including expansion of railways and ports. |
| **1974-1986** | - Transition to democracy post-Carnation Revolution.<br>- Decolonization leading to loss of overseas territories.<br>- Economic instability and high inflation.<br>- Nationalization of key industries followed by gradual privatization. |
| **1986-2000** | - Joining the European Economic Community (EEC) in 1986.<br>- Structural reforms and modernization of the economy.<br>- Focus on infrastructure development, including highways and airports.<br>- Growth in tourism, services, and foreign investment.<br>- Privatization of state-owned enterprises. |
| **2000s-Present** | - Adoption of the Euro in 1999.<br>- Economic challenges during the 2008 global financial crisis.<br>- EU bailout in 2011 with subsequent austerity measures.<br>- Gradual economic recovery with emphasis on technology, renewable energy, and tourism.<br>- Implementation of policies to promote innovation, entrepreneurship, and digital transformation.<br>- Focus on sustainable development and green technologies. |

## Economic Statesman(Statecraft)

> Here is the table of notable economic statecraft strategists in Portugal, ordered by their periods of influence:
> 

| Name | Period | Role/Position | Key Contributions and Policies |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Sebastião José de Carvalho e Melo (Marquis of Pombal)** | 1750-1777 | Prime Minister | - Implemented economic and administrative reforms post-1755 Lisbon earthquake.<br>- Modernized industry and trade policies, established manufacturing industries, and promoted wine production (Port wine). |
| **Teófilo Braga** | 1910-1911 | President of the Provisional Government, Prime Minister | - Led economic and social reforms during the First Portuguese Republic.<br>- Advocated for modernization and industrialization. |
| **António de Oliveira Salazar** | 1932-1968 | Prime Minister and Minister of Finance | - Established the Estado Novo regime with a focus on economic autarky.<br>- Implemented protectionist policies, state control of key industries, and infrastructure development. |
| **Marcelo Caetano** | 1968-1974 | Prime Minister | - Continued Salazar's economic policies with slight liberalization.<br>- Promoted industrial growth and infrastructure expansion.<br>- Managed economic challenges leading up to the Carnation Revolution. |
| **Mário Soares** | 1976-1978, 1983-1985 | Prime Minister | - Key figure in the transition to democracy post-Carnation Revolution.<br>- Focused on stabilizing the economy, securing EEC membership, and implementing structural reforms. |
| **João Salgueiro** | 1981-1983 | Minister of Finance | - Advocated for financial stability and economic reforms.<br>- Played a key role in preparing Portugal for EEC membership. |
| **Aníbal Cavaco Silva** | 1985-1995 | Prime Minister | - Led economic modernization and liberalization.<br>- Promoted privatization of state-owned enterprises and integration into the European Union.<br>- Fostered infrastructure development and foreign investment. |
| **Vítor Constâncio** | 1977-1980, 1985-1986 | Minister of Finance, Governor of the Bank of Portugal (2000-2010) | - Focused on monetary stability and financial regulation.<br>- Played a significant role in managing Portugal's integration into the Eurozone. |
| **José Sócrates** | 2005-2011 | Prime Minister | - Focused on technological innovation, renewable energy, and public works.<br>- Implemented policies to modernize infrastructure and education.<br>- Managed economic crisis leading to the EU bailout in 2011. |
| **Pedro Passos Coelho** | 2011-2015 | Prime Minister | - Implemented austerity measures and economic reforms following the 2011 EU bailout.<br>- Focused on fiscal consolidation, labor market reforms, and promoting economic recovery. |
| **António Costa** | 2015-present | Prime Minister | - Emphasized inclusive growth, social policies, and economic stability.<br>- Promoted digital transformation, green energy, and innovation.<br>- Managed economic response to the COVID-19 pandemic. |
| **Carlos Moedas** | 2014-2019 | European Commissioner for Research, Science and Innovation | - Promoted innovation and research within the EU.<br>- Focused on Horizon 2020 and strengthening Europe's global competitiveness in science and technology. |
