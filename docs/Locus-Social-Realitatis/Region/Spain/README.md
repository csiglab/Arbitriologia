## 📜 Economic History

| **1800–1890 (“Long 19th Century”)**                | - Spain remained largely agrarian; slow demographic growth and limited industrialization. ([Wiki Historia][1])  <br>- **Desamortización**: Liberal governments confiscated and sold Church and municipal lands, reshaping land ownership but not creating broad-based agricultural productivity gains. ([Oposinet][2])  <br>- Banking modernization: consolidation and crisis, establishment of Banco de España, financial institutions evolving. | **1890–1936 (First Globalization & Interwar)**     | - Spain participates in the first wave of globalization; export‑import patterns change. ([UAB Digital Document Repository][5]) <br>- Structural change in 1920s: some industrial growth, but still weak relative to other European powers. ([UAB Digital Document Repository][5]) <br>- Agrarian crisis & reform: efforts at agrarian reform during the Second Republic (e.g., 1932), but limited success. ([UAB Digital Document Repository][5]) <br>- Financial instability, political instability, the Great Depression’s effects. <br>- Civil War (1936–1939) disrupts everything.                                                                                                                                                  |

| **1959–1974 (“Spanish Miracle”)**                  | - Plan de Estabilización (1959): liberalization, stabilizing economy. ([Wikipedia][6]) <br>- High GDP growth (~6.5% annually) — the so-called “Spanish economic miracle.” ([Wikipedia][9]) <br>- Rapid industrialization: expansion in steel (Basque), shipbuilding, automotive (e.g., SEAT), petrochemicals. | **1975–1985 (Crisis and Transition)**              | - Oil shocks hit Spain; industrial crisis; unemployment rises. ([UAB Digital Document Repository][5]) <br>- Political transition after Franco: new institutions, social pacts (e.g., Pactos de la Moncloa 1977) to stabilize macroeconomy. | **1986–1998 (EU Integration)**                     | - Spain joins the European Economic Community (1986). ([UAB Digital Document Repository][5]) <br>- Structural reforms, liberalization, privatizations, renewed foreign investment. <br>- Sectoral shift: services (especially tourism) become major, industrial modernization. <br>- Use of EU structural funds for infrastructure and R&D.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |
| **1999–Present (Globalization, Crisis, Recovery)** | - Continued integration in global economy; increased FDI; more competitive industries (auto, renewable energy, ICT). <br>- 2008 financial crisis had heavy impact (real‑estate, construction). <br>- Recent pivot: industrial policy + “green” transformation, digitalization, EU Recovery Funds (e.g., NextGenerationEU). <br>- R&D and innovation growing, but productivity remains a structural challenge. <br>- High dependence on tourism; structural inequality; housing challenges.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |

## Human Capacity Profile

> España fue una potencia literaria, jurídica y filosófica en un país semiindustrial y científicamente subdesarrollado.
>

| Área | Situación |
| --- | --- |
| **Literatura** | Muy fuerte: siglo de escritores como Benito Pérez Galdós, Emilia Pardo Bazán, Unamuno, Valle-Inclán, Lorca. España era una potencia literaria mundial. |
| **Filosofía** | Notable: generación del 98 (Unamuno, Ortega y Gasset), pensamiento sobre decadencia nacional, europeización, modernidad. |
| **Derecho** | Avanzado: códigos civiles y penales modernos, fuerte tradición de juristas. Derecho español tenía prestigio en Hispanoamérica. |
| **Ciencia y Técnica** | Mucho más débil: pocos físicos, ingenieros o inventores mundialmente destacados. Pequeños núcleos científicos (Cajal, etc.), pero poco peso global. |
| **Industria** | Subdesarrollada: focos industriales pequeños (textil en Cataluña, minería en Asturias y Vizcaya, construcción naval en Cádiz y Cartagena), pero insuficientes para industrializar el país entero. |

## 🏭 Industrial Policy History

**Guiding Questions Recap:** How industries were promoted, state role, protectionism, sectors prioritized.

| **Period**                                              | **Key Policies and Developments**                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| ------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1700–1760 (Bourbon Reforms, early industrial thought)   | - Bourbon monarchs promoted economic modernization via **mercantilist policies**: tariffs, monopolies, and subsidies for key industries (textiles, shipbuilding, armaments). <br>- Creation of **royal manufactories** and support for industrial guilds. <br>- Theoretical influence from **mercantilist writers** advocating state-led economic promotion.                            |
| 1760–1800 (Late mercantilism / proto-industrialization) | - Promotion of **textile, glass, and paper industries** in Catalonia, Valencia, and Seville. <br>- State funding of technical schools, apprenticeship programs. <br>- Proposals for **industrial colonies** and infrastructural improvements (roads, canals) to facilitate commerce. <br>- Increasing awareness of the need for industrial development to reduce dependence on imports. |
| 1800–1850 (Post-Napoleonic recovery / liberal reforms)  | - Limited industrial promotion due to political instability, wars, and loss of colonies. <br>- Early **protectionist tariffs** to shield emerging industries. <br>- Proposals from thinkers on modernizing manufacturing and promoting mechanization.                                                                                                                                   |
| 1850–1898 (Industrial lag / selective modernization)    | - Some state support for **railways, mining, and basic metallurgy**. <br>- Public-private partnerships in infrastructure to support industrial clusters. <br>- Influence of **classical economic thought**: debates between laissez-faire and interventionism. <br>- Industrial policy mainly **ad hoc**, with localized development (Catalonia textile industry, Basque steel).        |
| 1898–1936 (Regeneration / interwar modernization)       | - Post-1898 “Regenerationism” promoted industrial modernization as a response to national decline. <br>- Support for **hydroelectric power, chemical industries, and mechanical engineering**. <br>- State-financed institutes of technology, industrial exhibitions, and technical education. <br>- Theoretical proposals on industrial rationalization.                               |
| 1936–1939 (Civil War)                                   | - Industrial policy halted; focus on war production. <br>- Destruction of industrial capacity in key regions.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| 1941–1959 (Autarky)                                     | - Using INI to build state-owned heavy industry. <br>- Import-substitution: limited imports, state control of industrial investment. <br>- Focus on “industrializing trilogy”: coal, steel, electricity.                                                                                                                                                                                |
| 1959–1974 (Development / Miracle)                       | - Plan de Estabilización opened the economy. <br>- Massive state investment via INI in big industrial firms (e.g., SEAT, Ensidesa, shipyards). <br>- Protectionist policies remained, but foreign capital began to play role; tourism was encouraged. <br>- Internal migrations supplied labor; urbanization.                                                                           |
| 1975–1985                                               | - Industrial restructuring in face of crisis. State started retrenchment. <br>- Political transition reduced interventionist policies; social pacts. <br>- Preparations for EEC integration.                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| 1986–2000s                                              | - EU structural funds used to modernize industry and infrastructure. <br>- Privatization of many former state-owned enterprises. <br>- Promotion of R&D, technology transfer, innovation. <br>- Emergence of industrial clusters and more globally competitive sectors.                                                                                                                 |
| 2010–present                                            | - Recovery plans (especially post-2008) with industrial policy tighter, more strategic. <br>- Emphasis on green transition, digitalization: connected industry, PERTEs (Strategic Projects for Recovery and Economic Transformation). <br>- Use of EU funds to support industrial modernization and innovation.                                                                         |
